Welcome!

The SFPD Dispatch dataset contains public information from the San Francisco Fire Department on dispatch information for 10,000 emergency calls, with call time and arrival times, multiple levels of location, call types, and dispositions from January 2018. This website serves to highlight some findings from this dataset- full data analysis scripts and code can be found in the links to the Jupyter Notebook above.

The Effect of Time of Day

Which times of the day are emergency responders slower to react to dispatch alerts?

→ Emergency responders had the lowest expected delay in marking themselves "en route" for emergency dispatches during evening, and naturally seemed to have the most lag during night hours. Here we plot the distribution of, specifically, the time difference between when the operator dispatches the unit and when the unit reporting that it is en route, for these two chunks of the day.
→ As the distribution suggests, we see late night hours (12 AM to 7 AM) have greater density towards the higher times, indicating slower response.


Two measures of central tendency, the mean and the median (used because it is robust to outliers), are shown in the table below for more information.

Average Time to Respond to Dispatch Alert for Portions of Day

Portion of Day Mean Time to Respond Median Time to Respond
Morning to Late Afternoon (8 AM to 3 PM) 1.25 min 0.70 min
Evening Hours (4 PM to 11 PM) 0.94 min 0.83 min
Night Hours (12 AM to 7 AM) 1.41 min 1.23 min

The Effect of Income

Which income band had the most emergency calls in the month of January?

How is the response and travel time for wealthier and poorer areas?

To answer this, we consult outside data to get median household incomes for zipcodes in San Francisco.
→ It turns out that emergency responders actually take longer to respond to emergency dispatches to wealthier districts and take longer to get on scene to these emrgencies overall.
While there may be a lot of confounding factors to this correlation, such as geographic circumstances (as we explore in Crime Correlation) it is reassuring to know that SF emergency responders aren't taking longer to respond to low income areas.
